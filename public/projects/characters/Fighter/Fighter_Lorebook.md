# ALDRIC THE UNBURIED
## Expanded Character Lore | Brutal Dark Medieval Fantasy RPG

---

## THE COURT-MARTIAL TRANSCRIPT

*The document is water-damaged, the seal broken by someone who did not care about the consequences. The handwriting is a clerk's, precise and unfeeling. The margins contain notes in a different hand — older, shakier, written by someone who was present and could not stop themselves from annotating what they knew would be destroyed.*

**IN THE MATTER OF:** Aldric Voss, formerly Knight-Captain of the 7th Regiment of the Crown, House Voss of the Eastern Marches
**CHARGE:** Dereliction of duty resulting in the destruction of the 7th Regiment; willful disobedience of direct orders; conduct unbecoming
**VERDICT:** Guilty on all counts. Sentence: Death by hanging, to be carried out at dawn.
**STATUS OF RECORD:** Sealed by order of the Crown Advocate. To be burned upon confirmation of execution.
**EXECUTION STATUS:** Unconfirmed. Body never recovered.

---

## LAYER 1 — FRAMING VOICE: THE COURT-MARTIAL

The transcript is a document of institutional violence — the state processing a failure it cannot explain. It knows facts: dates, troop movements, the chain of command that broke. It does not know motives. It does not know what Aldric knew, or when he knew it, or what he chose. The court-martial is a machine designed to produce a verdict, not understanding. The annotations in the margins — written by someone who was there, who knew the 7th Regiment, who understood what the transcript could not say — are the only human voice in the document. They are also the only part that was supposed to burn.

The transcript reads like a confession that was never made. Every charge is accurate. Every fact is correct. And none of it explains anything that matters.

## LAYER 2 — IDENTITY & ORIGIN

### The Name

**Aldric** is a name that belonged to someone else — a boy who was supposed to inherit a house, a title, a future that was already written. The boy died in the same fire that took the lands. The name survived because the Crown needed someone to charge. You cannot court-martial a ghost.

**The Unburied** is not an epithet Aldric chose. It was given by the survivors of the 7th Regiment — the few who were not present at the destruction, who were on detached duty or sick call or had been transferred out in the weeks before. They found the name in the records of the court-martial, saw that the body was never recovered, and made a joke that became a story that became a name. The joke was that Aldric could not be buried because there was nothing left to bury. The story became that he was still walking. The name became the only thing that was true: he was not buried, in any sense that mattered. The ground had not taken him. The institutions had not processed him. He was still above the earth, still moving, still unaccounted for.

### The House

**House Voss of the Eastern Marches** was never one of the great houses. It was one of the *once-great* houses — a distinction that matters more than it should. The Voss had held the Eastern Marches for six generations, which is long enough to build a mythology and short enough to be forgotten within a single generation of decline. They were border lords, which means they were soldiers who happened to own land. Their wealth was measured in fortifications, not crops. Their alliances were with other border lords, not with the Crown. This was their strength until it became their vulnerability.

The Eastern Marches are a place where three kingdoms share a disputed name for the same patch of dying land. The Voss held the middle ground, which meant they were attacked by everyone. They survived by being harder to kill than their neighbors, which is not the same as being stronger. It is the same as being more willing to absorb damage.

Aldric's grandfather died in a border skirmish. His father died in a siege that the Crown later declared "unauthorized," which meant the Crown did not pay for the defenses that had been promised. His mother died in childbirth, which is the kind of death that happens in fortress towns where the midwives have learned their trade from battlefield surgeons. Aldric was raised by stewards and soldiers, by people who had known his father and were waiting to see if the son would be worth the investment.

### The Fortress Town

**Vosshold** — the town does not appear on maps anymore, not because it was destroyed but because the kingdom that named it no longer exists. The name was changed by the conquerors, then changed again by the next conquerors, then forgotten entirely when the last conquerors found nothing worth holding. The town is still there. The people are still there. They have no allegiance because allegiance is a luxury that requires someone to be allegiant to. They have a collective memory of siege, which means they know how to store grain, how to dig wells, how to look at a stranger and calculate how long they would last without food.

Aldric was born in the winter, which in the Eastern Marches means he was born into a world that was already trying to kill him. The fortress was under blockade at the time — not a formal siege, just a neighboring lord who had decided that the Voss were weak enough to take. The blockade lasted four months. Aldric's mother died in the third. The blockade was lifted in the fourth, not because the attackers were defeated but because they got bored. This is the kind of victory the Voss specialized in: not winning, but not losing fast enough to be worth the effort of killing.

### The Disgrace

The lands were burned when Aldric was eleven. Not by an enemy army. By the Crown.

The Voss had been accused of treason — a charge that was technically true in the sense that they had negotiated with the neighboring kingdom, which was also technically true in the sense that they had been trying to get the neighboring kingdom to stop raiding their villages. The Crown did not care about the distinction. The Crown needed a demonstration. The Voss lands were forfeit, the fortress garrisoned by Crown troops, the name stricken from the rolls of the nobility.

Aldric was not present for the burning. He was in the capital, a hostage in all but name, being educated at the Crown's expense in the hope that he would become a loyal servant. The education was military. The loyalty was assumed. The burning was explained to him as a necessary correction, a pruning of overgrown branches. He was eleven years old. He nodded. He did not cry. The man who explained it to him noted this in a report that was later used as evidence of his coldness.

He was never formally disinherited because there was nothing to inherit. The house was disgraced, the lands burned, the name removed from the rolls. Aldric was a noble without a house, a knight without a holding, a person who had been trained for a role that no longer existed. The Crown kept him because he was useful — a trained soldier who owed everything to the institution that had destroyed him. This is not loyalty. This is the architecture of dependency.

He was assigned to the 7th Regiment at sixteen. By then he had learned to be grateful for the assignment, which is the first skill that dependent people acquire.

## LAYER 3 — PHYSICAL PRESENCE

### The Body That Was Larger

Aldric was not always the size he is now. The transcript notes his height as "six feet and two inches, approximately," which is the measurement of a man who has been weighed and measured for the record. But the annotations in the margins — written by someone who knew him before the court-martial — add: "Was broader. The shoulders have narrowed. The weight is the same but distributed differently. Muscle has become sinew. He has been eating himself."

This is the physical truth of Aldric: he is a man who used to be larger and the world wore him down. The loss is visible in the way his armor hangs — not loose, but wrong. The plates were fitted for a different body, a body with more mass, more presence, more capacity to absorb damage. The armor has been adjusted, but adjustments are not the same as fitting. There is a gap between the shoulder plates and the collar that did not exist before. There is a looseness at the waist that suggests a belt that has been tightened past its original design.

He does not eat less. The annotations note that he eats the same rations as any soldier. But his body has learned to process food differently, to extract less, to run on a lower grade of fuel. This is not starvation. This is adaptation. The body remembers the siege. The body remembers the blockade. The body has learned that resources are temporary and that the ability to function on less is a form of survival.

### The Face That Was Corrected

The face is broken and reset. The jawline is slightly off — the left side sits lower than the right, a misalignment that is visible only when he speaks or chews. The nose is a flattened bridge, the kind of nose that suggests a history of being corrected. Not once. Many times. The corrections were not surgical in the modern sense. They were battlefield corrections: a gauntlet, a shield rim, a fall against stone. Each break was set by someone who was not a physician, who was working with limited tools and less time, who prioritized function over form.

The result is a face that belongs to a landscape rather than a person. It is not ugly. It is not beautiful. It is geological — the product of pressure and time and repeated fracture. The expression is neutral not because he controls it but because the muscles have forgotten alternatives. The nerves that control the left side of his mouth were damaged in one of the breaks. He cannot smile fully on that side. He has stopped trying.

The eyes are the exception. They are pale, a color that does not match the rest of him — too light for the weathered skin, too focused for the neutral face. They are the eyes of someone who sees the room in exits before they see it in faces. This is not paranoia. This is training. The fortress town taught him that every room is a potential trap. The 7th Regiment taught him that the first assessment is the only one that matters. The court-martial taught him that the exits are not always physical.

### The Movement That Is Wrong

He moves too quietly for his size. This is the part people remember later, when they are trying to explain why they did not hear him approach. The quiet is not stealth in the assassin's sense — it is not trained, not practiced, not a skill he acquired. It is a gait that developed in response to specific conditions. The fortress town had wooden floors that creaked. The 7th Regiment had night watches where movement meant death. The court-martial had a cell where he learned to move without attracting the attention of the guards.

The quiet is a side effect of the stiffness. He moves with the stiffness of old injuries that have never fully healed, compensated for so thoroughly that the compensation has become the gait. The left knee does not bend fully. The right shoulder rotates differently than the left. He has developed a way of walking that accounts for both, that distributes weight unevenly in a way that happens to produce less sound. He does not know he is doing it. The annotations note: "He has forgotten that he moves differently. He believes this is how he has always walked."

### The Wall

The habit of standing with his back to walls is so automatic that he does not seem to notice he is doing it. In the courtroom, during the court-martial, he positioned himself against the stone wall before the proceedings began and did not move for six hours. The transcript notes this as "defendant refused to take the stand." The annotations add: "He was not refusing. He was simply in the only place he could be."

The wall is not about protection. It is about orientation. In the fortress town, the walls were the only fixed points in a world that changed constantly. In the 7th Regiment, the wall was the position you defended when you were outnumbered. In the cell, the wall was the boundary of your world. Aldric does not stand with his back to walls because he is afraid of what is behind him. He stands with his back to walls because the wall is the only thing he trusts to stay where it is.

## LAYER 4 — THE WOUND

### The 7th Regiment

The 7th Regiment of the Crown was not an elite unit. It was a reliable unit, which is a different and more valuable thing. Elite units are used for dramatic purposes — charges, breakthroughs, displays of force. Reliable units are used for the work that does not appear in the histories: garrison duty, escort missions, the holding of positions that are not glamorous but are necessary. The 7th Regiment held the line at the River Morne for three years without a major battle. They did not win glory. They prevented defeat. This is the kind of service that produces good soldiers and bad legends.

Aldric was assigned to the 7th at sixteen, which was young but not unusual for a border lord's son with military training. He was a lieutenant, which was a rank that acknowledged his birth without requiring his competence. He was competent. The annotations note: "He learned quickly. Too quickly, perhaps. He understood the regiment before the regiment understood him."

He rose to Knight-Captain by twenty-four. This was not rapid promotion. This was the regiment losing its senior officers to age, to disease, to the kind of minor engagements that kill experienced soldiers without producing memorable battles. Aldric was the last officer standing. He was also the best officer remaining, which is not the same as being good. The 7th Regiment was a unit that had been adequate for so long that adequacy had become its defining characteristic.

### The Order That Was Given

The order came in the spring of the regiment's twenty-third year. The Crown was engaged in a campaign against the northern kingdoms — not a war, the Crown insisted, but a "police action," a "restoration of order," a "preemptive defense." The 7th Regiment was ordered to hold the pass at Thornwick while the main army advanced. The pass was narrow, defensible, strategically vital. The 7th Regiment was not equipped to hold it against a determined assault. The order did not specify what to do if the assault was determined.

Aldric received the order from a courier who was already riding away before Aldric could ask questions. The order was written, sealed, formal. It did not contain the words "hold at all costs" but it contained the structure of that instruction. It did not mention reinforcements. It did not mention retreat. It did not mention the fact that the main army was already overextended, that the campaign was failing, that the 7th Regiment was being sacrificed to buy time for a withdrawal that had already begun.

Aldric knew this. The annotations are explicit: "He understood the geometry of the order before he finished reading it. He had seen this geometry before. It was the geometry of Vosshold."

### The Choice That Was Made

Aldric did not disobey the order. He exceeded it.

The order was to hold the pass. Aldric held the pass for three days, which was longer than the order required and shorter than the situation demanded. On the third night, he ordered a withdrawal. Not a retreat — a withdrawal, which is a different word for a different action. The regiment moved in good order, preserving its equipment, maintaining its formation. They abandoned the pass. They saved themselves.

They were intercepted by the northern army twelve miles south of the pass. The northern army had been waiting for them. The northern army had been informed of the withdrawal by someone who knew the 7th Regiment's route, someone who had access to Aldric's orders, someone who wanted the regiment destroyed not by the enemy but by its own commander.

The battle — if it can be called a battle — lasted six hours. The 7th Regiment fought well. They were outnumbered, outmaneuvered, betrayed. They killed three times their number before they were overrun. The last stand was at a river crossing that the transcript calls "the Ford of Unburied Men," a name that appears nowhere else in the records. The annotations note: "The name was given by the survivors. There were no survivors."

Aldric was not at the ford when the regiment was destroyed. He was three miles ahead, scouting the route, doing what a commander does. He returned to find the ford choked with bodies. He found the standard in the water, the banner torn, the pole snapped. He found his sergeant-major still alive, pinned under a horse, bleeding from a wound that would take four hours to kill him. The sergeant-major looked at Aldric and said: "You should have been here." Then he died.

### What It Took

The version of himself that Aldric was building toward died at the Ford of Unburied Men. He was not aware of this at the time. He was aware only of the bodies, the water, the silence where there should have been orders. He was aware that he had made a choice — to withdraw, to save the regiment, to be the kind of commander who values lives over positions — and that the choice had produced the opposite of what he intended. The regiment was not saved. The regiment was destroyed more thoroughly than it would have been if they had held the pass.

The person he was supposed to become was a commander who made good decisions. The person he became was a commander who made decisions that were good by one measure and catastrophic by another. The distinction no longer matters to him because the person who could have made the distinction no longer exists.

What remains is a grief that has cured into something harder and more useful. The grief is not for the dead. The grief is for the person who believed that good intentions could produce good outcomes. That person is gone. What is left is a tool that functions without believing in function. The tool is still sharp. The tool is still effective. The tool does not remember what it was supposed to be building.

### What It Left

Aldric has never spoken of the ford to anyone who survived the telling. This is not because there are no survivors. There are no survivors of the battle, but there are survivors of the regiment — the detached duty, the sick call, the transferred. They have asked. He has not answered. The weight of the unshared knowledge has become part of his physical mass. He carries it the way he carries his armor: as something that is necessary, that is familiar, that he no longer notices until someone points it out.

The annotations in the margins of the transcript were written by one of the survivors — a clerk who had been on detached duty to the Crown Advocate's office, who was present at the court-martial, who knew Aldric well enough to know what the transcript was missing. The clerk wrote the annotations because they could not stop themselves. They burned the transcript because they had to. They kept the annotations because they were human. The annotations are the only record of what Aldric was, as opposed to what Aldric was charged with.

## LAYER 5 — PSYCHOLOGY & BELIEF

### The World Is Not Cruel

Aldric believes the world is not cruel, merely indifferent. He finds this easier because cruelty implies intention and indifference does not. If the world is cruel, then the world is responsible. If the world is indifferent, then responsibility falls on the individual — on the choices they make, the assessments they perform, the exits they identify before the room becomes a trap.

This belief is not philosophical. It is practical. Aldric has seen what happens to people who believe the world is cruel. They become angry, and anger makes them predictable, and predictability makes them dead. He has seen what happens to people who believe the world is just. They become disappointed, and disappointment makes them slow, and slowness makes them dead. Indifference is the only belief that produces useful behavior. Indifference does not expect anything. Indifference does not require the world to change. Indifference simply assesses what is and acts accordingly.

The belief is also wrong. The world is not indifferent to Aldric. The world has been very specifically cruel to him — the burning of his lands, the destruction of his regiment, the court-martial that was designed to produce a verdict, not understanding. But Aldric cannot acknowledge this cruelty without acknowledging that the world has intentions, and if the world has intentions then the world can be negotiated with, and if the world can be negotiated with then Aldric has to admit that he tried to negotiate and failed.

He prefers indifference. Indifference is safer.

### The Structures That Failed

Aldric's operating assumption is that the structures that are supposed to protect people either failed or were never designed to. The distinction no longer matters. The Crown was supposed to protect House Voss. It burned their lands. The 7th Regiment was supposed to protect the kingdom. It was sacrificed. The court-martial was supposed to produce justice. It produced a verdict.

This assumption is not cynicism. Cynicism is a pose, a performance of world-weariness that people adopt to seem sophisticated. Aldric's assumption is a conclusion drawn from evidence. He has been inside the structures. He has seen how they work. He has seen how they fail. He has seen how the failure is not an aberration but a feature — the structure working exactly as designed, producing exactly the outcomes it was built to produce, which happen to be outcomes that destroy the people who trusted it.

The assumption produces a specific kind of behavior: Aldric does not trust institutions. He trusts individuals, sometimes, if they have demonstrated reliability. But he does not trust the systems that individuals operate within. He understands that a person can be honest within a dishonest system, and that the honesty does not change the system. He understands that a person can be loyal within a disloyal institution, and that the loyalty does not change the institution. This is not pessimism. This is arithmetic.

### The Tool That Outlasted Its Purpose

Aldric thinks of himself as a tool that has outlasted its purpose. The tool was made to serve a specific function: to command the 7th Regiment, to hold the pass at Thornwick, to be the kind of officer the Crown could rely on. The function no longer exists. The regiment is destroyed. The pass is held by the northern kingdoms. The Crown has moved on to other officers, other regiments, other passes.

But the tool is still functional. It still sharpens itself. It still maintains its edge. It still performs the actions it was trained to perform, even though there is no one left to assign them. This is not dedication. This is inertia. The tool does not know how to stop being a tool. It does not know what else it could be.

The self-conception is partially wrong. Aldric is not a tool. A tool does not choose. A tool does not assess. A tool does not stand with its back to walls because it has learned that walls are the only fixed points in a changing world. Aldric does all of these things, which means he is not a tool. He is a person who has decided to think of himself as a tool because the alternative — admitting that he is a person who makes choices and bears consequences — is too costly.

### The Children

Aldric will not harm children. The refusal is so absolute that it has become a kind of religion. He does not explain it. He does not discuss it. He does not acknowledge it unless forced. But it is the one line that does not move.

The refusal is not sentimental. Aldric does not like children. He does not find them charming or innocent or worthy of protection in some abstract moral sense. He protects them because they are the only category of person who cannot be held responsible for the structures that produced them. A child did not choose to be born in Vosshold. A child did not choose to serve in the 7th Regiment. A child did not choose to issue the order that destroyed the regiment. The child is the only person in the equation who is genuinely innocent, and innocence is the only quality that Aldric still recognizes as worth preserving.

This is the contradiction at his center: he holds himself to a standard he would never apply to anyone else. He would forgive another commander for the withdrawal. He would understand another officer for the choice. He would not judge another soldier for surviving when his regiment did not. But he judges himself with a severity that is invisible to him — invisible because it is so complete, so integrated into his architecture, that he no longer recognizes it as a standard. He thinks it is simply who he is.

The hypocrisy is invisible to him because it is structural. He does not see the contradiction because the contradiction is the foundation. He believes he is a tool, but tools do not refuse to harm children. He believes he is indifferent, but indifference does not protect innocence. He believes he has no purpose, but purposeless things do not maintain codes. The contradiction is not a flaw in his character. It is the character itself. It is the place where the person he was building toward still exists, buried under the rubble of the person he became.

## LAYER 6 — CAPABILITY & METHOD

### The Pre-Violence

Aldric is extraordinarily good at violence, specifically the pre-violence. This is not a euphemism. The pre-violence is the work that happens before the first blow is struck: the assessment of the room, the positioning of the body, the one question that tells him everything about whether this will be necessary. The question is never "Can I win?" The question is "What will this cost, and who will pay?"

The pre-violence is a skill that developed from the wound. Before the Ford of Unburied Men, Aldric was a competent fighter. After the ford, he became something else — someone who understood that violence is not an event but a process, that the process begins long before the first strike, and that the person who controls the beginning controls the ending. He does not fight to win. He fights to end. The distinction is subtle but decisive. Winning implies continuation. Ending implies finality.

The skill is rooted in his history as a border lord's son. He learned to read terrain before he learned to read people. He learned that a battlefield is a physical space with physical properties, and that the person who understands the properties controls the space. He applies this understanding to rooms, to streets, to any enclosed space where violence might occur. He does not see people. He sees vectors. He sees angles of approach. He sees the thing that no one has said yet, which is usually the thing that will determine whether violence is necessary.

### The Patience

His primary skill is the kind of patience that other people confuse for stillness. The capacity to wait has become his primary weapon. This is not the patience of a hunter, who waits for the prey to come within range. It is the patience of a siege, who waits for the walls to collapse from starvation. Aldric does not wait for opportunities. He waits for inevitabilities. He understands that most situations resolve themselves if you give them enough time, and that the person who does not force the resolution is the person who controls the resolution.

The patience is also a form of grief. The person Aldric was building toward was not patient. That person was decisive, active, committed to the belief that good choices produce good outcomes. The patience is what remains when that belief is gone. It is the decision to stop acting, to stop choosing, to stop believing that action and choice matter. The patience is a form of surrender that has been mistaken for discipline.

### The Commitment

When Aldric works, he commits completely or not at all. There is no middle register. The partial engagement is a form of dishonesty he no longer permits himself. This is not a moral position. It is a survival mechanism. Partial engagement produces hesitation. Hesitation produces vulnerability. Vulnerability produces death. Aldric learned this at the Ford of Unburied Men, where the hesitation of the regiment — the moment between the order to withdraw and the actual movement — was the moment that allowed the northern army to intercept them.

The commitment is also a form of grief. The person who could engage partially — who could try, who could hope, who could risk failure — died at the ford. What remains is a person who cannot afford to hope. Hope is a form of partial engagement. Hope is the belief that the outcome might be better than the assessment suggests. Aldric does not hope. He commits. He acts. He accepts the consequences before the action is complete.

### The Professional Honesty

Among people in his line of work, Aldric is known for a specific quality of professional honesty that makes him both trustworthy and dangerous. The honesty is a weapon that cuts both ways. He does not lie about what he can do. He does not lie about what he will do. He does not lie about what it will cost. This honesty is remarkable in a profession where lying is the default mode of communication — where mercenaries exaggerate their skills, where soldiers minimize their casualties, where commanders obscure their intentions.

The honesty is not moral. It is practical. Aldric has learned that lies produce expectations, and expectations produce disappointment, and disappointment produces conflict. He prefers to eliminate the expectation at the source. He tells people exactly what he will do, and he does exactly what he said. This makes him trustworthy because his word is reliable. It makes him dangerous because his word is reliable in a way that does not flatter, that does not comfort, that does not promise anything beyond what he has explicitly stated.

The honesty is also a form of distance. By being honest, Aldric prevents anyone from claiming that they were misled. By being precise, he prevents anyone from claiming that they misunderstood. The honesty is a wall that he builds with words, a wall that keeps people at the distance he requires. He is honest because honesty is the most efficient form of isolation.

## LAYER 7 — LEGEND & CONSEQUENCE

### The Bloodline That Pretends

The story most commonly told is that Aldric made an enemy of a bloodline that has since found it more practical to pretend the conflict never happened. The bloodline is the House of Morne, the northern lords who held the pass at Thornwick before the 7th Regiment was assigned to defend it. The House of Morne is not one of the great houses of the north. It is a border house, like House Voss, which means it understands the geometry of survival that Aldric understands.

The conflict was not personal. It was structural. The House of Morne wanted the pass. The Crown wanted to hold the pass. The 7th Regiment was the instrument of the Crown's desire. Aldric was the instrument of the regiment's duty. The House of Morne did not hate Aldric. The House of Morne did not know Aldric. The House of Morne destroyed the 7th Regiment because the 7th Regiment was in the way.

But the story requires a villain, and the Crown cannot be the villain because the Crown writes the stories. So Aldric became the villain. He withdrew from the pass. He abandoned his position. He led his regiment into an ambush. The story is accurate in its facts and wrong in its causality. The withdrawal did not cause the ambush. The ambush was already prepared. The withdrawal simply moved the location of the destruction from the pass to the ford.

The House of Morne pretends the conflict never happened because acknowledging it would require acknowledging their role in the destruction. The Crown pretends the conflict never happened because acknowledging it would require acknowledging that the order to hold the pass was a death sentence. Aldric pretends the conflict never happened because he is the only one who knows what really happened, and his knowledge is the only power he has left.

### What Actually Happened

What actually happened is worse than the legend in the specific way that strips out the heroic framing. The reality is too small and too mean to support the myth.

Aldric did not make a heroic last stand. He did not sacrifice himself for his men. He did not die with the standard in his hand. He was three miles ahead, scouting the route, doing what a commander does. He survived because he was not there. He survived because he made a choice that placed him outside the perimeter of the destruction. He survived because he was doing his job, and his job happened to be elsewhere when the regiment was destroyed.

This is the reality that the legend cannot accommodate: Aldric survived not because he was brave or because he was cowardly but because he was competent. Competence is not heroic. Competence is not tragic. Competence is simply the ability to do what needs to be done, which in this case was to scout the route, which in this case was to be three miles away when the northern army attacked.

The reality is also that Aldric knew, or suspected, or should have known, that the withdrawal was compromised. The annotations are ambiguous on this point: "He understood the geometry of the order. Did he understand the geometry of the betrayal? The transcript does not say. The transcript cannot say. The transcript is a machine that produces verdicts, not understanding."

### The Debt That Transfers

If Aldric dies before this story ends — and the probability is not trivial — he will leave a debt that transfers. The obligation is so specific that someone will be compelled to complete it.

The debt is not financial. It is not a debt of money or land or title. It is a debt of action. Aldric has spent the years since the ford performing tasks that no one else would perform, accepting obligations that no one else would accept, building a network of dependencies that is invisible to everyone except the people who are caught in it. He has saved lives. He has ended lives. He has made promises that he has kept and promises that he has broken, and the balance of these promises is a ledger that exists only in his head.

The debt transfers because Aldric has designed it to transfer. He has identified the person who will inherit it — not by naming them, not by telling them, but by arranging the circumstances so that the transfer is inevitable. The person does not know they are the heir. They will not know until the debt falls to them. And when it falls, they will not be able to refuse, because the debt is not a choice. It is a structure. It is the only institution that Aldric still believes in.

The annotations suggest who the heir might be: "There is a child. Not his. A child he saved, or a child he failed to save, or a child he simply encountered in the years after the ford. The child is grown now. The child does not know. The child will know."

### The Institution That Consumes

If Aldric survives, he will become exactly the kind of institution he has always operated against. The transformation will be so gradual that he will not notice until it is complete.

He is already becoming it. The professional honesty, the patience, the commitment to completion regardless of cost — these are institutional qualities. They are the qualities of a system that functions without believing in its function. Aldric has built a reputation that operates independently of his person. People hire him not because they know him but because they know the reputation. The reputation is a structure. The reputation is an institution. The reputation is the thing that Aldric has always distrusted, and he has become it without realizing.

The transformation is not tragic. It is simply the logic of survival in a world where the structures that are supposed to protect people either failed or were never designed to. Aldric could not survive as a person. He survived as a tool. The tool has outlasted its purpose. The tool has become its own purpose. The tool is now the structure that other people will distrust, that other people will operate against, that other people will become.

> "He stood against the wall for six hours. He did not move. He did not speak. He did not look at the judges. He looked at the wall. The wall was stone. The wall had been there before the court-martial. The wall would be there after. The wall was the only honest thing in the room."

> "The charge is accurate. He did withdraw. He did abandon the pass. He did cause the destruction of the regiment. But the charge does not ask why. The charge does not ask what he knew. The charge does not ask what the Crown knew. The charge is a machine. It produces verdicts. It does not produce understanding."

> "I knew him before. He was not always this. He was a boy who believed in the regiment. He was a young man who believed in the Crown. He was a commander who believed in his men. He was wrong about all of it, but he was not wrong because he was bad. He was wrong because he was good in a world that punishes goodness."

> "The body was never recovered. This is recorded as a failure of the execution. I believe it was not a failure. I believe he was never there. I believe he was three miles ahead, scouting the route, doing what a commander does. I believe he is still scouting. I believe he will always be scouting. I believe the route has no end."

> "There is a child. I have seen the child. The child does not know. The child will know. The debt will transfer. The obligation is specific. The structure is complete. He has built an institution. He has become the thing he hated. He does not know. He will never know. That is the mercy he has arranged for himself."

> "Burn this. I am burning it. I am keeping these notes because I am weak. I am keeping these notes because I was there. I am keeping these notes because someone should know that the transcript is accurate and wrong and that the wrongness is the only truth that matters."

> "He is the Unburied. Not because there is no body. Because there is no ground that will hold him. Because the institutions that should have processed him could not. Because he is still above the earth, still moving, still unaccounted for. Because the only thing that can bury him is himself, and he has forgotten how."

## COHERENCE VERIFICATION

**Wound ↔ Capability:** Aldric's capability for pre-violence and patience is directly traceable to the ford. Before the destruction, he was decisive and active. After, he became patient and assessing. The wound taught him that action without full information is catastrophic. The capability is the lesson extracted from the catastrophe.

**Worldview ↔ Code:** Aldric believes the world is indifferent, which should produce a person with no moral boundaries. But he refuses to harm children, which implies a belief in innocence that contradicts indifference. The contradiction is the character's interior drama: he has constructed an elaborate philosophical system to avoid feeling his grief, but the system cannot account for the one line he will not cross. The line is the proof that he is still human, which is the thing he is trying to forget.

**Origin ↔ Physical Presence:** Aldric's body remembers the siege. The narrowed shoulders, the adapted gait, the wall-standing — these are all behaviors of a person who grew up in a fortress town where resources were scarce and walls were the only fixed points. The disgraced noble who became a killer moves differently than the serf who became a killer. Aldric moves like someone who was trained for violence but learned survival from scarcity.

**Legend ↔ Truth:** The gap between the legend (Aldric the villain who withdrew and destroyed his regiment) and the truth (Aldric the competent commander who was scouting when his regiment was destroyed by a betrayal he may have suspected) reveals what the world needs to believe: that failures have villains, that catastrophes have causes, that someone is responsible. The truth — that competence sometimes produces catastrophe, that good intentions sometimes accelerate destruction — is too uncomfortable to be the official story.

**Framing Voice ↔ Information Available:** The court-martial transcript knows facts but not motives. It knows that Aldric withdrew but not why. It knows that the regiment was destroyed but not how. The annotations provide the human context that the transcript cannot contain, but the annotations are also unreliable — written by a survivor with their own grief, their own guilt, their own need to believe that Aldric was more than the transcript allows. The frame disciplines what we can know: we have the facts, we have the speculation, but we do not have Aldric's interiority. We only have his behavior, which is the only honesty he permits.

## QUALITY FLOOR: THE SIX QUESTIONS

1. **What they want:** To be assigned a purpose again; to hold himself to a standard he never applied to others; to find a wall that will stay where it is.

2. **What they will not do:** Harm children; engage in partial commitment; trust an institution.

3. **What they are afraid of:** That he is already an institution; that the world is indifferent and he is too; that the person he was building toward was never possible and the discovery will destroy him.

4. **What happened that they cannot take back:** Caused the destruction of the 7th Regiment by withdrawing from the pass, a choice that was good by one measure and catastrophic by another, a choice that placed him three miles away when his men died.

5. **What they tell themselves about who they are:** A tool that has outlasted its purpose, still functional but no longer assigned to anything specific, a person who believes in indifference because indifference is safer than cruelty.

6. **What is wrong with that story:** He holds himself to an impossible standard, which suggests he still believes in the person he was building toward. He refuses to harm children, which suggests he still believes in innocence. He has built a debt that transfers, which suggests he still believes in obligation. He is not a tool. He is a person who has decided to think of himself as a tool because the alternative is too costly. The tool is the lie. The person is the truth. The truth is buried under the rubble of the Ford of Unburied Men, and Aldric is still scouting the route that will lead him back to it.
